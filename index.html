Color legend theorist & influential teacher

Josef Albers, a German-born American artist and educator. He was a significant 20th-century art teacher in the United States.

Born in 1888 in Germany, Albers received practical training in various skills, including engraving glass, plumbing, and wiring.

He worked as a schoolteacher and received his first public commission in 1918. In 1920, he joined the Weimar Bauhaus and became a faculty member in 1922. After the institution's closure under Nazi orders in 1933, Albers emigrated to the United States and became the head of the painting program at Black Mountain College in North Carolina.

He taught prominent artists like Ruth Asawa and Robert Rauschenberg and invited contemporary American artists to teach in the summer seminar. In 1950, he left Yale University to head the design department, contributing significantly to its graphic design program. Albers' teaching methodology, prioritizing practical experience and vision in design, had a profound impact on postwar Western visual art.

He was also an active abstract painter and theorist, known for his series Homage to the Square and murals.
